DAW engine · the signal graph

Seeing the graph

Every sound the engine makes comes out of a graph: a handful of small processing nodes wired together, with one block of audio flowing through them from the sources to the output. Here it is, live — poke it and watch the signal change.

Nodes and wires

A node does exactly one job to a buffer of samples. A source (an oscillator) fills the buffer with a fresh tone. A transform (a gain) reshapes the buffer it's handed — here, scaling its volume. A mixer sums several buffers into one. A wire (an edge) just means "this node's output buffer is that node's input." String them together and you have a graph.

Try it: mute and un-mute the three oscillators and watch the mix node — and the final output below — get simpler or richer. Drag gain and watch the output's height shrink and grow while the shape stays the same. Every change you make is just editing the graph; the sound is whatever the graph currently computes.

Order matters

To fill one block, the engine walks the graph sources first — the green highlight sweeping left to right. That order isn't cosmetic: a node can't run until the buffers feeding it exist, so the mixer must wait for its oscillators, and the gain must wait for the mixer. Evaluate the nodes in that dependency order (engineers call it topological order) and every node finds its inputs ready. One pass through the graph = one block. Then it does it all again for the next block, 5.3 ms later.

Why a graph scales to a whole DAW

The reason this shape is worth building on: it grows without new concepts. A track is just a little sub-graph. A bus is a mixer node. An effect or plugin is one more transform node obeying the same contract as the gain. Add hundreds of them and the engine still does the same thing — walk the nodes in order, hand each one a buffer. The parameters you twist (like that gain slider) ride in on a separate path, the membrane, so the knobs never block the audio.
