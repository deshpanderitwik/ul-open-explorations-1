DAW engine · experiment 3

Why the average lies

A cook who's fast on average but freezes once an hour still ruins the recording — that freeze is an audible click. So we stopped trusting averages and built a stopwatch that watches the worst spoonful. Here's what it caught.

The one idea

Remember the deal: our cook must plate one spoonful of sound every 5.3 milliseconds, forever. Be late even once and the speaker runs dry — a pop, a click, a glitch in the recording. So the question that actually matters isn't "how fast is the cook on average?" It's "how bad is his slowest spoonful, ever?"

An average smooths all of that away. If 999 spoonfuls are quick and one takes forever, the average still looks great — and the average is exactly what would've let that one disaster slip through. So the new rig times every single spoonful and lines them up worst-first.

1 · Even a perfect cook has a bad day

First we watched a flawless kitchen — sixteen singers, no mistakes — for fifty thousand spoonfuls. Here's how long each one took. Almost all cluster around 29 µs (a rounding error against the 5,333 µs budget). But look at the far right: the slowest was 164 µs — about 5–6× the typical one.

50,000 spoonfuls · each bar = how many landed in that time bucket

We did nothing wrong — that long tail is the rest of the computer (the operating system briefly borrowing the cook for something else). The point: a single average would have reported "29 µs, all good" and hidden the existence of the slow ones entirely. You can't fix — or even fear — a worst case you never measured.

Somewhere in the low thousands of voices, one cook tips over the wall — and once the average is over, every spoonful is late. This ceiling is exactly what the later chapters fix: hire more cooks (run on several processor cores at once) and give them faster knives (chop eight numbers per stroke). For now, it's just good to know where the cliff is.

3 · The one forbidden errand

Now the real lesson. We sent the cook on one quick errand — fetch a fresh mixing bowl from the basement (in computer terms: ask the system for new memory) — on a tiny 0.1% of spoonfuls. Harmless-looking. Watch what it does.

Try it: flip errand on and the occasional skyscraper appears. Now here's the trap — at a relaxed deadline (a comfy buffer), even those skyscrapers stay under the line: zero clicks. Everything looks fine. Then switch the deadline to tight (the setting a musician uses to play live, where the system must respond almost instantly) — and those same spikes punch right through. The clicks counter starts climbing.

+1% · 18×

What the stopwatch actually measured

The errand moved the average spoonful by about +1% — utterly invisible. But it blew the worst spoonful up by ~18× (from 164 µs to ~2,950 µs). At a comfy buffer: 0 dropouts. At a low-latency buffer: real, audible clicks.

Why this is the whole ballgame

That tiny errand is invisible to the average and survives a relaxed deadline — so it sails through casual testing. Then a user drops to low latency to play live, and it becomes a click they can't explain. This is why the audio thread is forbidden from ever fetching memory, waiting on a lock, or touching the disk: one rare unbounded errand is harmless on paper and fatal in the tail.

It's also why this whole project optimizes for "never late" rather than "fast on average." The rig you just played with is now our permanent stopwatch — every future change gets weighed by its worst spoonful, not its typical one.

Next: hire more cooks. Split the kitchen across several processor cores so the choke ceiling rises — without ever breaking the never-late rule.
