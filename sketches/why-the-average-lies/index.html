DAW engine · experiment 3

Why the average lies

An audio engine that's fast on average but stalls once an hour still ruins the recording — that stall is an audible click. So we stopped trusting averages and built a rig that measures the worst block of audio it ever produces. Here's what it caught.

The one idea

Audio runs on its own dedicated thread, and that thread lives under a hard deadline. Every 5.3 milliseconds it has to fill one buffer — a block of 256 audio samples — and hand it to the sound card. Hand it over late and the card has nothing to play: silence where sound should be, which you hear as a click. Engineers call that miss a dropout (or xrun).

So the number that actually matters isn't the average time to fill a buffer — it's the worst one. An average hides exactly the thing that hurts you: if 999 buffers are quick and one takes far too long, the average still looks fine while that one late buffer is an audible glitch. So the rig times every single block and lines them up worst-first.

That single shift — judge the worst block, not the average — is the experiment. Everything below is the rig put to work: first as a plain yardstick (how many voices fit on one core?), and then for the part that actually matters — showing how one rare mistake stays invisible to the average while wrecking the worst case.

1 · Even a clean engine has a tail

First we ran a flawless signal graph — 16 oscillators mixed together, no mistakes — and timed 50,000 consecutive blocks. Here's how long each one took to render. Almost all cluster around 29 µs (a rounding error against the 5,333 µs budget). But look at the far right: the slowest block took 164 µs — roughly 5–6× the typical one.

50,000 blocks · each bar = how many landed in that render-time bucket

We did nothing wrong — that long tail is the rest of the system: the operating system briefly preempting our thread to run something else, a cache miss, a context switch. The way engineers pin this down is percentiles: the p99.9 (the worst block out of every thousand) was 72 µs, and the absolute max was 164 µs. A single average of "29 µs, all good" erases all of it — and you can't defend against a worst case you never measured.

Somewhere in the low thousands of voices, a single CPU core tips over the wall — and once the average block is over budget, every block is a dropout. That ceiling is exactly what the later rungs raise: spread the graph across multiple cores, and use SIMD (one instruction that runs the same math on eight samples at once). For now, it's just good to know where the cliff is — and to have a tool that finds it.

3 · One forbidden operation

Now the real lesson. We added one heap memory allocation on the audio thread — ask the system for a fresh 4 MB buffer and touch it — on just 0.1% of blocks. It looks harmless. The reason it's forbidden: an allocation has no bounded worst case. Usually it's quick, but it can drop into the operating system for fresh memory pages and take an eternity by audio standards. Try it: turn the allocation on and occasional spikes appear. Here's the trap — with a roomy 256-sample buffer (a 5.3 ms deadline), even those spikes stay under the line: zero dropouts. Everything looks fine in testing. Now switch to a 32-sample buffer (a 0.67 ms deadline — the low-latency setting a musician uses to play live) and the same spikes punch straight through. The dropout counter climbs.

+1% · 18×

What the rig actually measured

That one allocation moved the average block time by about +1% — utterly invisible. But it blew the worst block up ~18× (164 µs → ~2,950 µs). At a 256-sample buffer: 0 dropouts. At a 32-sample buffer: real, audible clicks.

Why this is the whole ballgame

That one allocation is invisible in the average and survives a roomy buffer — so it sails through casual testing. Then a user drops to low latency to play live and it becomes a click they can't explain. This is why the real-time audio thread may never allocate memory, wait on a lock, or touch the disk: any operation without a bounded worst case is harmless on paper and fatal in the tail. So how do we make sure it never happens? →

It's also why the whole project optimizes for the worst case ("never late") rather than the average ("fast on average"). The rig you just played with is now the permanent measuring stick — every future change is judged by its worst block, not its typical one.

In one sentence: this experiment built a stopwatch that judges the worst block instead of the average — then used it to find the single-core ceiling (Part 2) and, more importantly, to prove why one rare forbidden operation is fatal (Part 3).
