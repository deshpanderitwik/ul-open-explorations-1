The hungry speaker

To a computer, sound is just a long list of numbers — about 48,000 of them per second — each one a position for the speaker cone. The catch: the speaker is hungry and never stops. Feed it the next spoonful late and you hear an ugly click. So we work in spoonfuls (blocks of ~256 numbers). One spoonful buys us exactly 5.3 milliseconds to produce the next. That deadline is the whole game.

A higher pitch packs more cycles into the same spoonful. Drag it around — that's the speaker's diet, drawn in real time.

Talk #1 — the membrane (parameters)

Two workers with opposite rules. The UI thread handles your knobs and has no deadline. The audio thread (the cook) must plate a spoonful every 5.3 ms, no excuses. They must never touch the same data — so they meet at a tiny mailbox: the UI drops a sticky note, the cook glances at it at the start of each spoonful and keeps going. No waiting, ever. That hand-off is the membrane.

Talk #2 — the assembly line (audio)

A real instrument is a line of stations, each doing one job and handing its work down. A tone-maker (source) fills the spoonful; a volume (transform) reads that same spoonful and scales it. Every station wears the same trays (the Node trait), so the line can be rearranged on the fly — which a DAW absolutely needs.

Experiment 1: does a rearrangeable line cost us?

We raced a welded line vs. a plug-anything-in line (static vs. dynamic dispatch). Difference: within measurement noise. The flexible graph a DAW needs is essentially free — the whole 2-station line uses ~0.03% of the budget.

The mixer — many voices, one spoonful

Music is many voices at once. The mixer is the first station with many inputs and one output: several tone-makers sing, and it folds them into a single spoonful. Two clean sines summed make a lumpier wave — and that lumpiness is the chord.

Experiment 2: where should the spoonfuls live?

Textbook says mix into one shared bowl (an accumulator). We measured a cup per voice instead — and it was ~45% faster, flat across 1–64 voices. The shared bowl braids the summing into the un-vectorizable tone-making loop; separate cups let the chip add eight-at-a-time. "Always accumulate" was backwards at this scale.

16 voices, per block. The bowl's real edge turned out to be memory (1 buffer vs. N) and routing — not speed. Exactly the kind of folklore the stopwatch is here to overrule.

Two kinds of "talk," and what's next

Everything above is two ideas: the membrane (parameters flowing down from the relaxed world into the deadline-bound one) and the graph (audio flowing across a line of stations). A full DAW is a graph of stations whose knobs are fed by membranes — scaled up, parallelized, and hardened until the speaker never once goes hungry.

Next rung: the worst-case rig — stop measuring the cook's average speed and start measuring his worst spoonful ever, because that's the number that decides whether the speaker screams. "Never late" beats "fast on average."
